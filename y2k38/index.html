The 32-Bit Limitation

Why time runs out.

Legacy systems store time as a signed 32-bit integer—a count of seconds since January 1, 1970. The maximum capacity of this storage format is strictly limited.

Maximum Signed 32-Bit Integer
2,147,483,647

When the clock ticks one second past this number, the binary counter overflows. The sign bit flips, causing the date to wrap around instantly to 1901.

Historical Precedents

YouTube & Gangnam Style

In 2014, the video exceeded 2.14 billion views. YouTube's 32-bit counter broke, forcing a backend upgrade to 64-bit integers.

NASA Deep Impact

The probe's internal clock (counting tenths of a second) overflowed a 32-bit integer, causing a reboot loop that severed communication.

Boeing 787 Dreamliner

A directive required reboots every 248 days. The generator control units used a 32-bit counter for hundredths of a second, which overflowed at exactly 248 days.

The Solution: 64-Bit Architecture

Upgrading systems to store time as a 64-bit signed integer increases capacity exponentially.

With 64 bits, we won't run out of seconds for another 292 billion years—about 20 times the age of the universe.

Windows (x64), macOS, and iOS are already safe.
Linux 5.6+ kernel supports 64-bit time on 32-bit CPUs.
